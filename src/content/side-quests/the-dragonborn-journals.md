*From the Journal of Rey Meowsterio*

---

Gather round, children, and hear the tale of Meeko, the faithful.

It was a rainy, windswept morn when I left Rorikstead. At my side strode Lydia of Whiterun — sworn to carry my burdens — and, true to form, she carried both mine and her own. At my feet trotted Hilda: not just a goat, but *the* GOAT — *the greatest of all time*, horns high and ready.

We set out for the Horn of Jurgen Windcaller, taking the track north toward Morthal by way of Robber Gorge. The brigands there thought themselves fearsome. They learned otherwise. One I sent sailing from the bridge with the thunder of my voice; another met Hilda's horns and the river below. Lydia, doing what Lydia does best, took a rock to the head and lay sensibly out of the way for the remainder.

Beyond them we found a shattered cart and the quiet aftermath of an ambush: husband and wife gone together into Mara's keeping. There was no one left to punish, which is a poor sort of justice, but the road does not wait on grief.

Not long after, a yowling mutt set my ears ringing. We turned into the trees and found a hut, and by it a dog whose master had died of rockjoint — a miserable end, given how easily such a curse can be cured. The dog's name was Meeko. I asked him, plain as day, if he would join me. Lydia inclined her head. Hilda stamped once in agreement. There was only the matter of space.

Hilda took it like the champion she is. I sent her to the ghost plantation I had freed that very morning, to mind the fields as steward. She went without complaint, and Meeko came with us, tail high, a new oath written in his bones.

We passed through Morthal without pause — some churls prattled about wizards, but I had no time for their talk. Past blood-stained canyons, through frigid waters, and into a place that made my spine tighten: necromancers. The lowest sort. Men who would drag the honoured dead from Shor's halls and set them to gnaw at the world again for petty gain.

I went in with my war cry on my lips. Lydia's arrows sang from behind. Meeko ran at my side, faithful as breath. I raised my warhammer and brought it round in a great cleaving arc —

— and in that heartbeat, Meeko leapt.

He threw himself between me and the blow, into the path of my own strike. The hammer fell. Bone gave. He dropped at my feet, a small sound in his throat, life spilling quick into the dust.

The necromancer stared, then smiled — then began the foul work of calling Meeko back.

I moved before the spell could take hold. I caught the man and broke him between haft and forehead, snapping his neck like a twig. The magic guttered and died. The silence that followed was a mercy.

I knelt by Meeko. Faithful to the last, he had chosen me over his own breath. There are debts a man cannot pay, only carry.

So I carried him, as Lydia carries my burdens, as Hilda carries the fields. I laid him to rest as best I could, and sent him on to his master, where no necromancer's hand can reach.

---

Remember him, children. Meeko, the faithful.
