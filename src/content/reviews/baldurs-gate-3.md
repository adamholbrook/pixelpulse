The original Baldur's Gate games are sacred texts to RPG fans. So when Larian Studios — riding high on the success of Divinity: Original Sin 2 — announced they were taking on the franchise, expectations were complicated. Three years of Early Access later, the full release of Baldur's Gate 3 doesn't just live up to the legacy. It surpasses it.

## The Illusion of Complete Freedom

The most impressive thing about BG3 isn't any single mechanic — it's the seamless illusion that you can do anything. Want to romance the vampire spawn in your party? Befriend the villainous cult leader instead of fighting him? Stage a one-person coup against a literal god? The game says yes to all of it, and the consequences ripple outward in ways you don't expect until 80 hours later when a character you met briefly in Act 1 shows up to help you in your darkest moment.

## Combat That Rewards Creative Thinking

Larian's turn-based combat system, built on D&D 5e rules, rewards lateral thinking. Every environment is a puzzle. Barrels of explosive oil, high ground advantages, wet enemies and lightning spells — the combat sandbox is so rich that no two encounters feel identical even on a second playthrough.

## Characters Worth Caring About

Shadowheart. Astarion. Gale. Karlach. The companion roster in BG3 is the best in the genre's history. Each character has a fully realized arc, their own secrets, and dialogue that reacts to your actions in genuinely surprising ways. The voice acting is uniformly stunning.

## The Third Act Problem

BG3 stumbles slightly in its final chapter. The city of Baldur's Gate itself, while beautifully realized, buckles a little under the weight of all the threads Larian needs to resolve. Some storylines feel rushed, and the performance takes a hit. But even a slightly stumbling BG3 is still a masterpiece.

A crowning achievement in RPG design. Essential.
