Astro Bot is a fantastic platformer that makes full use of the PS5 controller and haptic feedback, delivering a nostalgia-driven experience — reminding older gamers of games from their youth and consoles from years ago whilst showcasing these entries as a worthwhile exploration for a younger audience.

You play as Astro Bot, a lone bot survivor after the Mothership is downed by a rogue alien. You must traverse the galaxy to reunite bots and hardware components with the Mothership, defeat the Space Bully Nebulax, and save the galaxy. The game combines narrative-driven exploration with nostalgia: a gacha machine unlocks special bots representing characters from PlayStation's gaming catalogue, each evoking iconic weapons, enemies, and surroundings from their home games.

## Structure

Much like the platformers of old — Spyro, Crash — Astro Bot consists of seven hub worlds: a main hub for meta progression, five for world progression, and a secret hub. Each hub contains worlds, some locked by progression, and each world clearly states its contents upfront — how many bots to rescue, how many puzzle pieces to find — appearing in order as you progress, allowing you to backtrack if you're missing something.

Each world has a themed design; there's some crossover as you might expect, but for the most part the variation is good. Enemy design is simple, with the variance mostly being by theme, but the game does a good job of slowly introducing new enemies as you progress and become more comfortable with the mechanics — starting with simple bots that charge you and building to shapeshifting enemies that keep you on your toes. The worlds themselves are often straightforward linear paths, but are satisfying platformers, blending mastery of the controls with a forgiving reset system: no lives, frequent checkpoints, minimal loss of progression. As a gaming dad of a toddler with a penchant for achievement hunting, the two rarely go hand in hand — Astro Bot delivers on both, making it a strong option for time-strapped gamers looking to dip in for an hour or two and knock out a world or a hub at a time.

## Challenge Worlds

For gamers seeking a challenge, there are also challenge worlds — hidden levels that truly demand mastery of the controls. These are often one-shot levels with no checkpoints, requiring patience and precision, quite often under time pressure. They add a welcome break from the slightly longer pacing of a full world, with the goal simply being a clean completion of the level in one run.

## The DualSense

The haptic feedback is sublime. Not only the expected — gyro controls for steering the flight mechanics, trigger resistance on firing gun-style projectiles — but the rumbles and clanks are incredibly satisfying. The plinking of rain on Astro Bot's metal body is ASMR by any other name. It's truly a masterclass in what the PS5 is capable of, something I wish more games explored and used rather than treating as a novelty.

## Where It Stumbles

Not all is well in paradise. Beat-based levels don't always hit right — the music is well designed and a delight to enjoy on loop, but it often dissociates from the environment. In games like DDR or *Crypt of the NecroDancer*, there's an expectation that enemies and hazards move with the beat, allowing the player to keep time without having to monitor every obstacle. Here, hazards sometimes move at a different pace, leading to jarring dissociation and unsatisfying resets. Some checkpoints are placed too far from difficult sections, feeling punishing compared to the often plentiful earlier ones. The Lost Galaxy portals feel suitably placed — not too obvious, always locatable for the eagle-eyed — but their positioning is questionable for the time-strapped, sometimes appearing mid-level and necessitating either significant backtracking or a full revisit.

The difficulty manages well overall, growing gradually and culminating in late-game masterclasses in mechanics. Some levels do frustrate though. The Crumble levels — requiring you to break the floor to drop enemies — work well, growing harder as enemy types and combinations become more challenging. Some don't land, however: one combination involving a Volcano enemy that breaks the platform of its own volition renders the level engagement obsolete. Luckily, moments like this are few and far between.

## Overall

An excellent entry in the PlayStation catalogue. It showcases gaming history well, with bonus worlds dedicated to PlayStation's most popular entries and content for all ages. The modular design means you can smash a hub in an evening, spend twenty minutes 100%-ing one world, or cruise through in ten. A solid game that reminds you that platformers don't need to be stress-inducing timed speedruns against a manic backbeat. It lands well with all kinds of player: time-strapped parents will appreciate the modularity of hitting one world at a time, young gamers taking their first steps won't feel overwhelmed, the cash-strapped will get plenty of mileage, and achievement hunters will enjoy a well-judged trophy list.
